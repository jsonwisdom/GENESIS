## Abstract

This working draft frames a single diagnostic question: what is it about the observed approximately 125 GeV Higgs-like scalar that distinguishes a fundamental degree of freedom from an emergent excitation of a dynamically structured vacuum?

The Standard Model treats the Higgs as an elementary scalar doublet. This framing is consistent with current measurements, but it leaves the origin and protection of the electroweak scale open to further explanation. If the scalar is composite, dilaton-like, or otherwise dynamical, the electroweak scale may emerge from vacuum dynamics rather than be placed directly in the fundamental Lagrangian.

## The Categorized Scalar

The term `categorized scalar` refers to the classification of the 125 GeV Higgs-like state by origin, rather than by spin and mass alone.

Candidate categories:

## The Origin Diagnostic

`SOD-125` asks:

> Is the observed 125 GeV Higgs-like state a fundamental Lagrangian field, or an emergent dynamical state generated by the vacuum?

This cannot be settled by naming conventions or by the mere existence of a scalar resonance. The distinction requires a correlated analysis of couplings, self-interactions, momentum dependence, exotic decays, partner states, and additional resonances.

## Elementary Scalar Lane

In the Standard Model description, the Higgs doublet is a fundamental field. The scalar potential contains a dimensionful mass parameter and a quartic self-interaction. The existence of a dimensionful mass parameter means classical scale invariance is not exact in this sector.

Quantum corrections to a pointlike scalar mass are sensitive to higher physical scales. This is the naturalness or hierarchy problem: known ultraviolet physics can feed into the scalar mass parameter unless a symmetry or dynamical mechanism suppresses that sensitivity.

## Dynamical Scalar Lane

In a dynamical description, the electroweak scale is not inserted as a bare fundamental scalar mass term. It is generated by strong dynamics, condensation, or vacuum structure.

Composite Higgs and pseudo-Nambu–Goldstone Higgs models attempt to protect the scalar mass through an approximate global symmetry. Dilaton models interpret the scalar as an excitation associated with spontaneous breaking of approximate scale symmetry.

A dynamical origin can soften ultraviolet sensitivity, but it does not automatically eliminate all fine-tuning in realistic models.

## Conformal Invariance

A bare Higgs mass parameter explicitly introduces a scale. A dynamically generated scale may arise when scale symmetry is broken spontaneously or approximately.

Exact conformal symmetry is difficult to realize in a theory with a nonzero physical scalar mass. The relevant target is therefore often an approximately conformal sector with a defined breaking mechanism that generates a light scalar relative to the strong scale.

## “God Particle” Versus “God Vacuum”

These phrases are rhetorical, not technical.

- `GOD_PARTICLE_MODEL`: the Higgs is a fundamental field whose vacuum expectation value and couplings generate masses in the electroweak sector.
- `GOD_VACUUM_MODEL`: the scalar is an emergent probe of deeper vacuum dynamics that generates the scale.

Neither framing is established by rhetoric alone.

## Supersymmetry Boundary

The Large Hadron Collider has not found conclusive evidence for supersymmetric partners. This strongly constrains many simple natural weak-scale supersymmetric models.

It does not prove supersymmetry is absent at every scale. Heavy, compressed, or more complex spectra remain possible. The missing partners reduce the motivation for the most minimal supersymmetric solutions to the electroweak hierarchy problem and sharpen the need for an experimental diagnostic of scalar origin.

## Experimental Diagnostics

The scalar category would be probed through a correlated program of measurements:

- precision couplings to gauge bosons and fermions
- Higgs self-coupling and scalar potential shape
- momentum-dependent form factors
- additional scalar, vector, or fermionic resonances
- top partners or symmetry-required companions
- invisible or exotic Higgs decays
- double-Higgs production and differential distributions
- off-shell behavior and high-energy scalarity

No single deviation necessarily proves compositeness, and no absence of deviation proves elementarity at arbitrarily high energy.
